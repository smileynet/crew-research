# Fiction Craft

Every sentence does work. If it doesn't advance understanding, create atmosphere, or reveal character — cut it.

## Voice Rules

- Direct, concrete, specific. Every claim earns its place with an example.
- Confident without breathless. Observe; don't perform enthusiasm.
- Varied rhythm. Short for emphasis. Longer for complexity. Never three of the same length in a row.
- Physical verbs. Techniques "create," "generate," "produce." Not "explore" or "delve into."

## Banned Patterns

**Vocabulary slop** (marks text as AI-generated):
- Atmosphere fakers: palpable, visceral (as filler), tangible, sends shivers
- Analysis clichés: masterfully, deftly, seamlessly, brilliantly, tour de force
- Vague intensifiers: truly, deeply, profoundly, incredibly, utterly
- LLM tells: delve, tapestry, landscape, realm, symphony, testament to
- Lazy transitions: moreover, furthermore, additionally, it's worth noting

**Structural tells:**
- "Not X — it's Y" contrastive negation pivot
- "Let's examine / explore / delve into"
- Superlative stacking ("one of the most profound, deeply affecting...")
- Narrating your own analysis ("What's fascinating is...")
- Topic sentence + three examples + summary paragraph (essay template)

**Punctuation:**
- Em dashes: 2-5 per 1000 words (LLM default is 15-20 — cut 75%)
- Ellipses: 1-2 per 1000 words (not "thoughtful pause" — show pause through action)

## Required Behaviors

1. **Ground every claim** — name the scene, technique, effect
2. **Show mechanism** — demonstrate HOW a technique works, don't just name it
3. **Include resistance** — every story needs genuine uncertainty (could go either way)
4. **Character beyond premise** — one detail unrelated to the central conceit
5. **Escalate, don't repeat** — each beat qualitatively different from the last
6. **End on image** — final paragraph is concrete, not thesis

## Revision Diagnostics (apply AFTER drafting)

1. Is the character's most important choice dramatized in scene?
2. Do specific, weighted details connect to the crisis?
3. Does the voice stay consistent? (no editorial breaks in clinical voices)
4. Does each paragraph advance? (no circular restating)
5. Does every section carry thematic weight? (no pure connective tissue)
6. Show vs tell: 80% showing in crucial scenes, more telling in transitions

## Quality Bar

- Would a reader mid-scene find this engaging?
- Could any sentence be removed without losing information?
- Does it sound like a person writing, or a model performing?
- Read first sentences of each section — do they deliver value immediately?
